A Newcomer's Guide to Eating Well in a New City

For students & new professionals · 6 min read

Moving to a new city is exhilarating and exhausting in equal measure. Between finding a place to live, learning your way around, and starting a new job or college, one basic need quietly becomes a daily struggle: food. In your first few weeks it's tempting to live on delivery apps and canteen snacks — but that gets expensive and unhealthy fast. Here's a calm, practical plan to sort out good daily meals early, so food becomes one less thing to worry about.

Week one: stabilise the basics

You don't need to solve everything at once. In your first week, aim for just two reliable meals a day — usually lunch and dinner. These are the meals that, left unplanned, drain your wallet through impulse ordering. Breakfast and snacks can stay flexible for now.

Why a tiffin is the newcomer's best friend

A home-cooked tiffin service solves several newcomer problems at once:

  • Predictable cost — a fixed monthly amount instead of unpredictable delivery bills.
  • Home-style comfort — familiar, simple food when everything else feels new.
  • No cooking setup needed — useful when your kitchen is still a single induction plate.
  • Time saved — no daily decision about what and where to eat.

How to find one quickly

Use a directory rather than relying only on word-of-mouth, which can be slow when you're new. On DabbaMap you can search by your area or pincode and immediately see home cooks near your flat or office, filter by veg/non-veg/Jain and cuisine, and compare prices. Sort by distance to find the closest options — proximity matters most for hot, on-time food.

Pick based on your hostel, PG, or office

Decide where you most need the meal delivered. If you're out all day, a lunch tiffin to your office and a separate dinner near your PG might work better than both from one cook. Many people mix and match: one provider for weekday lunch near work, another for dinner near home.

Always trial before you commit

As a newcomer you don't yet know which flavours and portion sizes suit you here. Order a trial meal or two before locking into a monthly plan. It costs little and saves you from a month of food you don't enjoy. Use anonymous messaging to ask about schedule, delivery timing, and whether they can adjust spice levels.

Budgeting your food sensibly

A realistic monthly food budget for someone new to a city, eating mostly home-style:

  • Two tiffin meals a day: roughly ₹2,800–₹6,000/month combined.
  • Breakfast & chai: ₹800–₹1,500/month if kept simple.
  • Weekend eating out: whatever you choose to allow — this is your flexible bucket.

Locking the daily meals into a tiffin keeps the big, recurring cost stable, so the rest of your budget breathes.

Settling in

Once your daily meals are handled, a new city becomes far more manageable. You'll have more energy, more money, and more headspace to actually enjoy it — the street food, the restaurants, and the endless variety — on your own terms rather than out of daily necessity.
