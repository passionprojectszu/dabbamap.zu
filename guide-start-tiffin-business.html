How to Start a Home Tiffin Service and Earn Money

For home cooks · 10 min read

Every day in India, millions of people — students, working professionals, migrants from other cities — are eating badly because they don't have access to good home-cooked food. At the same time, hundreds of thousands of home cooks have the skill, the kitchen, and the time to feed them. If you love cooking and want a reliable income from home, starting a tiffin service is one of the most practical and rewarding ways to do it.

This guide walks you through everything: what you actually need to start, how to price your food, how to get your first ten subscribers, and how to build from there.

What a home tiffin business actually is

A tiffin business is simple at its core: you cook fresh food daily and deliver or hand it over to a fixed set of subscribers who pay you monthly or weekly. Most home cooks start with 5–10 subscribers and grow steadily as their reputation spreads. At 30 subscribers paying ₹2,500–₹3,500/month each, you are looking at ₹75,000–₹1,05,000 a month in revenue. Many experienced tiffin cooks in cities like Mumbai, Pune, Delhi, and Bengaluru operate at this scale from a single home kitchen.

Unlike running a restaurant, there is no rent, no large staff, no heavy equipment investment, and no uncertain footfall. Your income is predictable because subscribers pay in advance.

What you need to get started

1. A functional home kitchen

You don't need a commercial kitchen. What you do need: a gas stove with at least two burners, a pressure cooker, good stock pots, and enough vessels to cook for 10–15 people. A refrigerator for storing ingredients is essential. Keep your workspace clean, organised, and pest-free — this is the foundation of a trustworthy kitchen.

2. Good packaging

Your tiffin containers make a first impression. Invest in stainless steel tiffin boxes or good-quality reusable containers — they look professional, keep food hot longer, and are environmentally better than disposable packaging. If you use disposable containers, choose sturdy ones that won't leak.

3. A clear menu and schedule

Decide what you'll cook before you take your first subscriber. Will you offer lunch, dinner, or both? Will you cook Monday to Saturday, or all seven days? Plan a weekly menu that rotates — ideally repeating no dish within a week. Variety is one of the most common reasons subscribers continue long-term. Write this out on paper before you begin.

4. A way to communicate and collect payment

WhatsApp works perfectly for most home tiffin cooks: it's where your subscribers will message you, confirm orders, and share feedback. Collect payment via UPI (GPay, PhonePe, Paytm) at the beginning of the month. Never allow large arrears — if someone hasn't paid for more than two weeks, pause their service until they settle up.

How to price your tiffin

Pricing is where most beginners go wrong — either undercharging and burning out, or overcharging before they've built a reputation. A sensible approach is to work backwards from your costs:

ItemEstimated cost per meal
Raw ingredients (vegetables, dal, roti flour, oil, spices)₹25–₹45
Gas/fuel₹4–₹8
Packaging₹3–₹8
Delivery (if applicable)₹10–₹30
Total cost₹42–₹91

At 10 subscribers, your production costs drop per unit because you buy ingredients in bulk. A reasonable starting price in a metro city is ₹80–₹130 per meal for a basic tiffin (2 rotis, 1 sabzi, dal, rice). In Tier-2 cities, ₹60–₹100 is common. Monthly plans at 26 meals/month (Mon–Sat) typically run ₹2,000–₹3,200.

Don't compete purely on price — compete on quality and consistency. Subscribers who stay long-term do so because the food is good, not because it's the cheapest.

Getting your first 10 subscribers

The first ten are the hardest. After that, word of mouth does most of the work. Here's what actually works:

  • Start with people you know. Neighbours, relatives of neighbours, colleagues of family members. Offer them a free trial meal and let the food speak. People who like your food will tell others.
  • Residential buildings near you. Post a simple handwritten or printed flyer on the notice board of apartment complexes within 1–2 km. Focus on buildings with a lot of working professionals, bachelors, or PGs — your core audience.
  • List on DabbaMap. Creating a free listing on DabbaMap puts your dabba in front of people actively searching for home-cooked food in your area. Fill your profile completely — describe what you cook, your daily menu style, your price, and your delivery area. Listings with good descriptions get significantly more enquiries.
  • Local Facebook and WhatsApp groups. Most residential areas have local buy-sell groups. A simple post — "I'm a home cook in [area] offering tiffin from ₹XX/month, starting with trial meals" — with a photo of your food regularly gets responses.
  • Nearby offices and co-working spaces. Many working professionals in offices nearby need daily lunch. If you can deliver reliably before 12:30–1:00pm, office areas are excellent territory.

Managing your business as you grow

Once you cross 15–20 subscribers, things get more complex and you need simple systems.

  • Keep a subscriber list. Names, addresses, contact numbers, dietary requirements, and payment status — all in one notebook or spreadsheet. This is your business record.
  • Batch your cooking. Cook dal and rice in large quantities first, then manage individual rotis or sabzis. The more organised your kitchen sequence, the less time you spend per meal.
  • Hire help for delivery. At 20+ subscribers, delivering everything yourself is difficult. A local delivery person on a bicycle or two-wheeler, paid per delivery or on a part-time salary, can expand your range significantly.
  • Rotate your menu weekly. Subscribers notice and appreciate variety. Keep a rotation chart so you never repeat the same sabzi within a 5-day period.

FSSAI registration: what you need to know

Under Indian food law, any food business — including a home kitchen — should have basic FSSAI registration. For small home-based tiffin services, this is a Basic FSSAI Registration (not a full licence), costs around ₹100/year, and can be applied for online through the FSSAI FoSCoS portal. While many small home cooks operate without it initially, registration builds trust with customers, is required if you scale up, and gives you a legal footing. It is strongly recommended once you are taking regular paid orders.

The honest truth about tiffin businesses

A home tiffin service requires consistency above all else. Subscribers depend on you showing up with food every day — that reliability is what they're paying for. Days when you're unwell, when ingredients are unavailable, or when something else comes up are the real challenges. Build rest days into your plan from the start (most cooks take Sundays off), communicate changes early, and keep a small buffer in your subscriber count so one or two cancellations don't affect your income sharply. The cooks who succeed long-term are not necessarily the best chefs — they are the most reliable ones.
